# FAME 2

**Fractional Flow Reserve versus Angiography for Multivessel Evaluation 2**

---

## Quick Facts

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Year** | 2012 (initial), 2018 (5-year) |
| **Journal** | NEJM |
| **DOI** | 10.1056/NEJMoa1205361 |
| **ClinicalTrials.gov** | NCT01132495 |
| **Bottom Line** | FFR-guided PCI + OMT superior to OMT alone for stable CAD with ischemia |

---

## Design

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Type** | Randomized controlled trial |
| **N** | 888 (1,220 enrolled, 332 in registry) |
| **Centers** | 28 sites, Europe and North America |
| **Population** | Stable CAD with ≥1 stenosis and FFR ≤0.80 |
| **Mean Age** | 63 years |
| **Randomization** | 1:1 |

### Key Design Feature
- All patients underwent FFR measurement
- Only those with FFR ≤0.80 randomized
- FFR >0.80 patients entered registry (OMT only)

### Arms

| Arm | N | Intervention |
|-----|---|--------------|
| PCI | 447 | FFR-guided PCI + Optimal Medical Therapy |
| OMT | 441 | Optimal Medical Therapy alone |

---

## Endpoints

### Primary Endpoint
**Composite of death, MI, or urgent revascularization at 2 years**

### Key Secondary Endpoints
- Individual components of primary endpoint
- Any revascularization
- Angina status (CCS class)
- Quality of life

---

## Results

### Primary Endpoint — Initial Publication (Stopped Early)

**Trial stopped early for efficacy at median 7 months follow-up**

| Outcome | PCI + OMT | OMT Alone | HR (95% CI) | P-value |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **MACE (death, MI, urgent revasc)** | **4.3%** | **12.7%** | **0.32 (0.19-0.53)** | **<0.001** |

### Component Analysis (7 Months)

| Outcome | PCI + OMT | OMT Alone | P-value |
|---------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Death | 0.2% | 0.7% | NS |
| MI | 3.4% | 3.2% | NS |
| **Urgent revascularization** | **1.6%** | **11.1%** | **<0.001** |

**The benefit was driven almost entirely by urgent revascularization**

### 2-Year Outcomes

| Outcome | PCI + OMT | OMT Alone | HR | P-value |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-----|---------|
| Primary MACE | 8.1% | 19.5% | 0.39 | <0.001 |
| Urgent revascularization | 4.0% | 16.3% | 0.23 | <0.001 |
| MI | 5.0% | 6.0% | NS | — |
| Death | 0.7% | 1.4% | NS | — |

### 5-Year Outcomes (2018)

| Outcome | PCI + OMT | OMT Alone | HR (95% CI) | P-value |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **MACE** | **13.9%** | **27.0%** | **0.46 (0.34-0.63)** | **<0.001** |
| Death or MI | 8.1% | 12.0% | 0.66 (0.43-1.00) | 0.049 |
| Death | 5.1% | 5.2% | NS | — |
| MI | 4.6% | 8.0% | 0.56 | 0.01 |
| Any revascularization | 13.9% | 31.3% | <0.001 | — |

**5-year NNT = 8** for primary endpoint

### Angina Relief

| Metric | PCI + OMT | OMT Alone |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Angina-free at 1 year | 72% | 58% |
| CCS Class 0-I at 1 year | 85% | 75% |

---

## The Registry Arm (FFR >0.80)

| Outcome (2 Years) | Registry (No PCI) |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| MACE | 3.0% |
| Death or MI | 2.8% |

**Key insight:** FFR >0.80 lesions have excellent outcomes without revascularization — validates FFR deferral

---

## Clinical Impact

### What Changed
1. **FFR became standard of care** for intermediate lesions
2. **Reimbursement** — FFR wires became billable
3. **Deferral validation** — confidence to defer FFR-negative lesions
4. **Guideline Class I** — FFR for PCI decision-making (ACC/AHA, ESC)

### The FAME Legacy
| Trial | Question | Answer |
|-------|----------|--------|
| FAME 1 | FFR-guided vs angio-guided PCI | Fewer events, fewer stents with FFR |
| **FAME 2** | FFR-guided PCI vs OMT | PCI better for FFR+ lesions |
| FAME 3 | FFR-guided PCI vs CABG | PCI non-inferior debated |

---

## Limitations

1. **Early termination** — stopped at 7 months, may overestimate effect
2. **Soft endpoint driving results** — urgent revascularization is subjective
3. **Unblinded** — operators knew allocation, could influence revasc decisions
4. **DES era** — 2nd generation DES used; even better outcomes expected now
5. **Selection bias** — highly selected stable CAD population

---

## Controversies

### "Urgent Revascularization" Criticism
- Critics argue urgent revasc is a soft endpoint influenced by unblinding
- Patients in OMT arm who returned with angina were revascularized (as expected)
- Counter: 5-year data shows MI reduction (4.6% vs 8.0%), a hard endpoint

### ISCHEMIA Trial Context
- ISCHEMIA (2020) showed no benefit of routine revascularization in stable CAD
- But ISCHEMIA excluded left main, included patients without confirmed ischemia
- FAME 2 specifically enrolled FFR-positive (proven ischemia)
- **Synthesis:** Revascularization benefits ischemia, but you must prove ischemia first

### OMT Definition
- "Optimal" medical therapy was protocol-defined
- Critics argue real-world OMT may be suboptimal
- Counter: Even if OMT imperfect, reflects clinical reality

---

## FAME 2 vs ISCHEMIA: Reconciliation

| Factor | FAME 2 | ISCHEMIA |
|--------|--------|----------|
| Ischemia confirmation | FFR ≤0.80 (all) | Stress test (variable) |
| Left main | Excluded | Excluded |
| Population | FFR-positive | Moderate-severe ischemia on imaging |
| Result | PCI benefit | No routine PCI benefit |
| Interpretation | Proven ischemia → PCI helps | Unproven ischemia → PCI doesn't help |

**The lesson:** FFR-confirmed ischemia predicts PCI benefit; anatomic or imaging-based selection does not

---

## Clinical Pearls

1. **FFR ≤0.80 + stable CAD = PCI indication** — that's the FAME 2 message
2. **FFR >0.80 = safe to defer** — 3% MACE at 2 years in registry
3. **Urgent revasc drove early results** — but MI reduction emerged at 5 years
4. **NNT = 8 at 5 years** — meaningful benefit
5. **Don't extrapolate to ACS** — FAME 2 was stable CAD

---

## Key Takeaway for Practice

> FAME 2 established the principle: **if you can prove ischemia with FFR, revascularization improves outcomes.** The debate shifted from "should we do FFR?" to "FFR should be routine for intermediate lesions." Deferring FFR-negative lesions is safe; treating FFR-positive lesions is beneficial.
