# CLOSE

**Patent Foramen Ovale Closure or Anticoagulation vs. Antiplatelets after Stroke**

---

## Quick Facts

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Year** | 2017 |
| **Journal** | NEJM |
| **DOI** | 10.1056/NEJMoa1705915 |
| **ClinicalTrials.gov** | NCT00562289 |
| **Bottom Line** | PFO closure superior to medical therapy for cryptogenic stroke with high-risk PFO features |

---

## Design

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Type** | Randomized controlled trial |
| **N** | 663 |
| **Centers** | 34 French sites |
| **Population** | Cryptogenic stroke (age 16-60) with PFO + atrial septal aneurysm OR large shunt |
| **Mean Age** | 43 years |
| **Key Feature** | Required high-risk PFO anatomy |
| **Randomization** | 1:1:1 (three arms) |

### Key Inclusion Criteria (Strict Selection)
- Cryptogenic ischemic stroke within 6 months
- PFO on TEE with EITHER:
  - **Atrial septal aneurysm (ASA)** — ≥10mm excursion
  - **Large shunt** — >30 microbubbles in left atrium

### Arms

| Arm | N | Intervention |
|-----|---|--------------|
| PFO Closure | 238 | Device closure + DAPT 3 months, then ASA |
| Anticoagulation | 187 | Warfarin or DOAC |
| Antiplatelet | 238 | ASA or clopidogrel |

---

## Endpoints

### Primary Endpoint
**Stroke recurrence (fatal or nonfatal)**

### Key Secondary Endpoints
- TIA
- Systemic embolism
- Procedural complications
- New-onset atrial fibrillation

---

## Results

### Primary Endpoint: Stroke Recurrence

| Arm | Events | Rate | HR vs Antiplatelet |
|-----|--------|------|-------------------|
| **PFO Closure** | **0** | **0%** | **—** |
| Anticoagulation | 3 | 1.5% | NS |
| **Antiplatelet** | **14** | **6.0%** | **Ref** |

**PFO closure: 0 strokes vs 14 in antiplatelet arm**

| Comparison | HR (95% CI) | P-value |
|------------|-------------|---------|
| **Closure vs Antiplatelet** | **0.03 (0.00-0.26)** | **<0.001** |
| Anticoag vs Antiplatelet | 0.44 (0.11-1.48) | 0.18 |

### **NNT = 17** at 5 years (to prevent one stroke)

### Secondary Endpoints

| Outcome | Closure | Anticoag | Antiplatelet |
|---------|---------|----------|--------------|
| TIA | 3.4% | 3.3% | 4.6% |
| Procedural complication | 5.9% | — | — |
| New atrial fibrillation | 4.6% | 0.9% | 0.5% |

**A-fib with closure:** Mostly periprocedural, transient

### Safety

| Event | Closure |
|-------|---------|
| Device-related complication | 5.9% |
| Major bleeding | 0% |
| Device erosion | 0% |

## Context: The PFO Trilogy (2017)

Three PFO trials published simultaneously in NEJM:

| Trial | N | Result | Unique Feature |
|-------|---|--------|----------------|
| **CLOSE** | 663 | **Positive** | Required high-risk anatomy (ASA or large shunt) |
| RESPECT (extended) | 980 | Positive | Long-term follow-up of prior neutral trial |
| DEFENSE-PFO | 120 | Positive | Small, high-risk PFO features |
| Gore REDUCE | 664 | Positive | Lower-risk PFO included |

**Meta-analysis (all trials):** HR 0.30 for stroke with closure

## Why CLOSE Succeeded (When Earlier Trials Failed)

### Earlier Negative Trials

| Trial | Year | Result | Why Negative |
|-------|------|--------|--------------|
| CLOSURE I | 2012 | Negative | STARFlex device (inferior), unselected PFO |
| RESPECT (initial) | 2013 | Neutral | Underpowered, short follow-up |
| PC Trial | 2013 | Neutral | Small N, underpowered |

### CLOSE Design Advantages

1. **High-risk PFO only** — ASA or large shunt required
2. **Extended follow-up** — median 5.3 years
3. **Experienced operators** — established French structural centers
4. **Modern devices** — predominantly Amplatzer

---

## Clinical Impact

### What Changed
1. **Class I recommendation** — PFO closure for cryptogenic stroke with high-risk features (AHA/ASA 2021)
2. **Standard of care shift** — closure now default for appropriate patients
3. **Neurologist-cardiologist collaboration** — essential for screening
4. **Device market expansion** — multiple approved PFO occluders

### Current Guidelines (2023-2024)

| Criteria | Recommendation |
|----------|----------------|
| Age 18-60, cryptogenic stroke | Evaluate for PFO |
| PFO + ASA or large shunt | Class I for closure |
| PFO without high-risk features | Class IIb (weaker evidence) |
| Age >60 | Individualized (less clear benefit) |

---

## Patient Selection: The CLOSE Criteria

### Strong Indication (Class I)
- [ ] Age 18-60
- [ ] Truly cryptogenic stroke (workup complete)
- [ ] PFO on TEE (bubble study positive)
- [ ] PLUS at least one:
  - [ ] Atrial septal aneurysm (≥10mm excursion)
  - [ ] Large shunt (>30 microbubbles)

### RoPE Score Integration

| RoPE Score | PFO-Attributable Fraction | Closure Benefit |
|------------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| 7-10 | 72-88% | High |
| 5-6 | 40-56% | Moderate |
| 0-4 | 0-20% | Low |

**Higher RoPE = more likely PFO caused stroke = more benefit from closure**

---

## Limitations

1. **Open-label** — patients knew allocation
2. **High-risk PFO only** — doesn't apply to all PFO
3. **French population** — may not generalize
4. **Three-arm design** — anticoag arm smaller, underpowered for that comparison
5. **New AF signal** — 4.6% in closure arm

---

## Controversies

### Atrial Fibrillation Risk
- 4.6% new AF with closure vs 0.5% with antiplatelet
- Mostly periprocedural, often transient
- Long-term AF risk unclear
- Counter: 4.6% AF vs 6% stroke — trade-off favors closure

### Anticoagulation vs Closure
- CLOSE underpowered for this comparison
- NAVIGATE-ESUS (2018): rivaroxaban neutral in cryptogenic stroke (but not PFO-specific)
- Current practice: closure preferred over anticoag for high-risk PFO

### "Complete" Workup Requirement
- Cryptogenic = extensive workup negative
- Requires: echo, telemetry/monitor, carotid imaging, hypercoag panel
- Real-world: workups often incomplete

---

## Clinical Pearls

1. **Zero strokes in closure arm** — dramatic result
2. **ASA or large shunt = high-risk** — these are the patients who benefit
3. **RoPE score >6** — strengthens indication
4. **Age <60** — strongest evidence here
5. **Post-procedure AF** — real but usually transient; monitor for it

---

## Key Takeaway for Practice

> CLOSE proved that **the right PFO in the right patient** should be closed. High-risk features (ASA, large shunt) identify the subset where paradoxical embolism is likely the mechanism. **Zero strokes in the closure arm** is as definitive as it gets. For young cryptogenic stroke patients with high-risk PFO anatomy, closure is now standard of care.

> **The catch:** Truly cryptogenic requires complete workup. Don't close a PFO until you've ruled out AF, carotid disease, and other sources.
