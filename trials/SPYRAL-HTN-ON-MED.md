# SPYRAL HTN-ON MED

**Renal Denervation in the Presence of Antihypertensive Medications**

---

## Quick Facts

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Year** | 2018 (pilot), 2020 (pivotal) |
| **Journal** | Lancet (2018), Lancet (2020) |
| **DOI** | 10.1016/S0140-6736(18)30951-6 |
| **ClinicalTrials.gov** | NCT02439775 |
| **Bottom Line** | Renal denervation reduces BP on top of medications — the comeback after SYMPLICITY HTN-3 |

---

## Design

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Type** | Randomized, sham-controlled, blinded trial |
| **N** | 80 (pilot), 331 (pivotal) |
| **Centers** | Multiple international sites |
| **Population** | Uncontrolled hypertension despite 1-3 antihypertensive medications |
| **Mean Age** | 54 years |
| **Randomization** | 1:1 RDN vs sham |

### Key Design Improvements Over SYMPLICITY HTN-3
- **Sham control** — rigorous blinding with renal angiography in both arms
- **Standardized medication protocol** — controlled medication use
- **Consistent RDN technique** — four-quadrant ablation pattern required
- **Fewer lesions threshold** — minimum ablation requirements
- **Drug monitoring** — verified medication adherence

### Arms

| Arm | N | Intervention |
|-----|---|--------------|
| RDN | 166 (pivotal) | Spyral catheter, multi-electrode RF ablation |
| Sham | 165 (pivotal) | Renal angiography only |

---

## Endpoints

### Primary Endpoint (Pivotal)
**Change in 24-hour ambulatory systolic BP at 6 months**

### Key Secondary Endpoints
- Change in 24-hour diastolic BP
- Change in office systolic/diastolic BP
- Safety (composite of death, renal artery events, kidney function decline)
- BP control at various timepoints

---

## Results

### Primary Endpoint: 24-Hour Ambulatory SBP (6 Months)

| Outcome | RDN | Sham | Difference | P-value |
|---------|-----|------|------------|---------|
| **24h SBP change** | **-9.0 mmHg** | **-2.3 mmHg** | **-6.8 mmHg** | **<0.001** |

### Secondary BP Endpoints

| Parameter | RDN | Sham | Difference |
|-----------|-----|------|------------|
| 24h DBP change | -5.9 mmHg | -1.7 mmHg | -4.2 mmHg |
| Office SBP change | -9.7 mmHg | -2.6 mmHg | -7.1 mmHg |
| Office DBP change | -5.2 mmHg | -1.8 mmHg | -3.4 mmHg |
| Daytime SBP | -8.9 mmHg | -1.8 mmHg | -7.1 mmHg |
| Nighttime SBP | -8.1 mmHg | -2.9 mmHg | -5.2 mmHg |

### Subgroup Consistency

| Subgroup | RDN Better? | Interaction |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Age ≥55 vs <55 | Yes (both) | NS |
| Baseline SBP higher vs lower | Yes (both) | NS |
| Number of meds (1-2 vs 3) | Yes (both) | NS |
| eGFR > vs ≤ median | Yes (both) | NS |

**Consistent benefit across subgroups**

| Major adverse events | 1.4% | 1.5% |
| Renal artery stenosis | 0% | 0% |
| eGFR decline >25% | 2.1% | 3.3% |
| Death | 0% | 0% |

---

## Why SPYRAL Succeeded Where SYMPLICITY HTN-3 Failed

### SYMPLICITY HTN-3 Problems

| Issue | SYMPLICITY HTN-3 | SPYRAL |
|-------|------------------|--------|
| Sham procedure | Not rigorous (just angio) | Rigorous (full procedure simulation) |
| Ablation technique | Inconsistent, operator-dependent | Standardized four-quadrant pattern |
| Medication adherence | Unmonitored, variable | Verified with drug testing |
| Lesion delivery | Variable, often inadequate | Multi-electrode, more complete |
| Patient selection | Very resistant HTN (5+ meds) | Moderate HTN (1-3 meds) |
| Placebo effect | Large in control | Minimized by sham |

### The Key Lesson

> **SYMPLICITY HTN-3 was a procedural failure, not a scientific failure.** 
> The concept was sound — the execution was flawed.

---

## Clinical Impact

### What Changed
1. **Rekindled interest** in renal denervation after SYMPLICITY HTN-3 killed the field
2. **FDA pathway reopened** — Medtronic pursuing US approval again
3. **Technique standardized** — consistent ablation patterns required
4. **Patient selection refined** — moderate HTN, not extreme resistant HTN
5. **Medication framework** — RDN as adjunct, not replacement

### Where RDN Fits

| Setting | Role |
|---------|------|
| Uncontrolled on 1-3 meds | Adjunct to add BP reduction |
| Medication intolerance | Alternative approach |
| Non-adherence | "Fire and forget" |
| Resistant HTN (≥4 meds) | Still being studied |

## Limitations

1. **Modest BP reduction** — 6.8 mmHg is clinically meaningful but not dramatic
2. **6-month endpoint** — durability data still maturing
3. **Industry-sponsored** — Medtronic funded
4. **Renal function selection** — excluded eGFR <45
5. **Technique-specific** — results may not generalize to all RDN devices

---

## Controversies

### Is 6.8 mmHg Enough?
- **Epidemiology:** Every 5 mmHg SBP reduction → ~10% CV event reduction
- **Counter:** Patients on meds could just add another pill
- **Defense:** RDN benefit is additive and permanent (no adherence needed)

### Mechanism Questions
- Efferent vs afferent nerve disruption
- Incomplete denervation — is there reinnervation?
- Which patients benefit most?

### Cost-Effectiveness
- Single procedure vs lifetime pills
- Assumes adherence problem
- No outcomes trial (yet)

## Clinical Pearls

1. **6.8 mmHg reduction** — clinically meaningful, similar to adding one antihypertensive
2. **SYMPLICITY HTN-3 was technique failure** — not proof RDN doesn't work
3. **Standardized ablation essential** — four-quadrant, main + branch arteries
4. **Best candidates:** Uncontrolled on 1-3 meds, adherence concerns, pill burden
5. **Not a cure** — most patients still need medications

---

## Key Takeaway for Practice

> SPYRAL HTN-ON MED redeemed renal denervation after SYMPLICITY HTN-3's failure. With **rigorous sham-control and standardized technique**, RDN achieves **~7 mmHg additional BP reduction** on top of medications — equivalent to adding another antihypertensive. This isn't about replacing pills; it's about **adding permanent BP reduction** for patients who can't or won't achieve control with medications alone.

> **The conversation:** "There's a procedure that can lower your blood pressure by about 7 points on top of your medications. It's a one-time treatment that lasts. You'll likely still need pills, but fewer or lower doses. It's an option if pills aren't getting you controlled."
