# PARTNER 3

**Placement of Aortic Transcatheter Valves 3**

---

## Quick Facts

| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Year** | 2019 |
| **Journal** | NEJM |
| **DOI** | 10.1056/NEJMoa1814052 |
| **ClinicalTrials.gov** | NCT02675114 |
| **Bottom Line** | TAVR superior to surgery in low-risk severe AS at 1 year |

---

## Design

| Parameter | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Type** | Randomized controlled trial |
| **N** | 1,000 |
| **Centers** | 71 sites |
| **Countries** | USA, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Japan |
| **Population** | Severe aortic stenosis, low surgical risk (STS-PROM <4%) |
| **Mean Age** | 73 years |
| **Mean STS Score** | 1.9% |
| **Randomization** | 1:1 |

### Arms

| Arm | N | Intervention |
|-----|---|--------------|
| TAVR | 503 | SAPIEN 3 (transfemoral preferred) |
| SAVR | 497 | Conventional surgical AVR |

---

## Endpoints

### Primary Endpoint
**Composite of death, stroke, or rehospitalization at 1 year**

### Key Secondary Endpoints
- All-cause mortality at 30 days and 1 year
- Stroke at 30 days and 1 year
- Rehospitalization at 1 year
- New-onset atrial fibrillation
- New permanent pacemaker
- Aortic regurgitation ≥ moderate

---

## Results

### Primary Endpoint (1 Year)

| Outcome | TAVR | SAVR | Difference | HR (95% CI) | P-value |
|---------|------|------|------------|-------------|---------|
| **Death, stroke, or rehospitalization** | **8.5%** | **15.1%** | **-6.6%** | **0.54 (0.37-0.79)** | **0.001** |

**NNT = 15** (treat 15 patients with TAVR instead of SAVR to prevent 1 event)

### Component Analysis (1 Year)

| Outcome | TAVR | SAVR | HR (95% CI) |
|---------|------|------|-------------|
| All-cause death | 1.0% | 2.5% | 0.41 (0.14-1.17) |
| Stroke | 1.2% | 3.1% | 0.38 (0.15-1.00) |
| Rehospitalization | 7.3% | 11.0% | 0.65 (0.42-1.00) |

### 30-Day Outcomes

| Outcome | TAVR | SAVR |
|---------|------|------|
| Death | 0.4% | 1.1% |
| Stroke | 0.6% | 2.4% |
| New pacemaker | 6.6% | 4.1% |
| New A-fib | 7.7% | 35.4% |
| Life-threatening bleeding | 0.8% | 3.6% |
| AKI stage 2/3 | 0.4% | 1.7% |

### Echocardiographic Data (1 Year)

| Parameter | TAVR | SAVR |
|-----------|------|------|
| Mean gradient (mmHg) | 12.8 | 11.2 |
| Aortic regurgitation ≥ moderate | 0.6% | 0.5% |
| Valve area (cm²) | 2.0 | 2.1 |

## Long-Term Follow-Up

### 5-Year Data (Presented 2023)

| Outcome | TAVR | SAVR | P-value |
|---------|------|------|---------|
| Death or disabling stroke | 22.8% | 27.2% | 0.07 |
| Valve reintervention | 0.9% | 0.8% | NS |
| Endocarditis | 1.4% | 1.0% | NS |

**Key finding:** No signal for excess structural valve deterioration with TAVR at 5 years

---

## Clinical Impact

### What Changed
1. **FDA approval** of SAPIEN 3 for low-risk AS (August 2019)
2. **Expanded TAVR indication** to essentially all symptomatic severe AS (except contraindications)
3. **Heart Team discussions** shifted from "is the patient too sick for surgery?" to "which approach does the patient prefer?"
4. **Age consideration** became more nuanced — TAVR not just for elderly

### Guideline Updates
- **2020 ACC/AHA VHD Guidelines:** TAVR Class I for ≥80 years or ≤10-year life expectancy; Class IIa for younger low-risk patients
- **2021 ESC/EACTS:** Similar recommendations, emphasizing Heart Team decision

---

## Limitations

1. **Highly selected population** — excluded bicuspid valves (most), small annuli, horizontal aorta
2. **Short follow-up** — 1 year primary endpoint; durability assessment requires longer
3. **Single valve platform** — SAPIEN 3 only; may not generalize to all TAVR devices
4. **SAVR arm outcomes** — may not reflect all surgical centers (experienced sites only)
5. **Industry-sponsored** — Edwards Lifesciences funded the trial

---

## Controversies

### The Durability Debate
- Critics argue 1-year follow-up insufficient for low-risk patients who may live 15-20+ years
- Surgical valves have proven 15-20 year durability
- Counter: 5-year data shows no excess SVD signal
- Unknown: Valve-in-valve feasibility long-term

### Lifetime Management
- Low-risk patient at age 65 may need reintervention
- TAVR-in-TAVR vs TAVR-to-SAVR considerations
- Coronary access concerns with future procedures

### Cost-Effectiveness
- TAVR devices more expensive than surgical valves
- Shorter hospital stay offsets some cost
- ICER analyses show borderline cost-effectiveness in low-risk

## Clinical Pearls

1. **The 6.6% ARR** is one of the largest treatment effects in modern cardiology trials
2. **New pacemaker rate higher with TAVR** (6.6% vs 4.1%) — important for patient counseling
3. **A-fib reduction dramatic** (7.7% vs 35.4%) — underappreciated benefit of avoiding sternotomy
4. **"Low risk" in this trial** = STS 1.9% — truly healthy patients

---

## Key Takeaway for Practice

> PARTNER 3 fundamentally changed TAVR from a "last resort for high-risk patients" to "default option for many with severe AS." The question is no longer "can we do TAVR?" but "should we do TAVR vs surgery for this specific patient?" — driven by anatomy, age, patient preference, and lifetime management considerations.
